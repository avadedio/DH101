# Reflections

## Reflection 1 — Week 6: What do we gain/lose when machines "read" literature for us?

When machines "read" literature for us, we gain and lose meaning depending on the context. On one hand, we can gain a broader understanding of literary meaning that may feel more societal and collective. A machine reading of a text can challenge our own assumptions and subject position and expose us to patterns, structures, and interpretations that we might not pick up on our own. Especially if we are too deeply engrossed in our own perspective, this kind of analysis can help us see the world and the text through a new lens. However, some aspects of reading a text can also be lost; if we rely too heavily on machine based interpretations, we may weaken our own ability to develop interpretive or analytical tools. Literature is inherently personal and individual, and part of its value is its sense of ambiguity and opportunity for interpretation. When machine systems like ChatGPT quickly transform texts into a clear emotional storyline or assign symbolic meaning, it can feel like it has given us the answer. While this can expand our perspective in certain ways, it may also compromise the individual and more reflective process of a personal reading.

## Reflection 2 — Week 3: What does "authenticity" mean when identity is co-constructed with AI?

When identity is co-constructed with AI, authenticity is fundamentally compromised. Even if you work closely with an AI to obtain and reflect the most authentic identity possible, it can never fully represent the complexity and nuance of lived human experience. Even the most specific and individual details become averaged representations of that living identity; the most unique features or characteristics are reduced to what is socially legible and recognizable. Therefore, authenticity can still be expressed and represented to an extent, but that expression is flattened through dominant understandings of identity rather than personal meaning. Importantly, AI also cannot capture the unique interaction between the different aspects that together form identity.

## Reflection 3 — Week 13: Do you imagine AI futures as utopian, dystopian, or in-between? What do your visions reveal about us today?

I imagine AI futures as being a mix of dystopian and utopian because of its range of possibilities for use depending on a multitude of factors. First, we need to question and work toward understanding how much users may know about AI in general, but also their understanding of specific platforms and their unique abilities and limitations when imagining the future. As AI is becoming further integrated into our society and further institutionalized today, it does and will continue to blur the line between our personal and professional lives which I think is a big concern. For example, we see AI being integrated into the workplace and even being required in some cases, but we also see a rise in discourse about AI boundaries with personal and sensitive topics such as mental health. This reveals that automation risks blurring lines and boundaries around authenticity, safety, and identity.
